1. Executive Summary

The Australian National University (ANU) has experienced a consistent decline in its overall world university rankings across all major providers since its peak in 2016–2017. In the QS World University Rankings, ANU dropped from #19 (2016) to #32 (2026); in the THE World University Rankings, from #47 (2017) to #73 (2026). This decline is not primarily driven by deterioration in ANU's absolute performance. Its QS overall score of 87.4 in 2026 was actually the highest in six years, but rather by the rapid rise of competitors, particularly from Asia (China, India, Malaysia, and Thailand).

At the subject level, the picture is more nuanced. ANU maintains world-leading positions in humanities and social sciences (Philosophy #8, Anthropology #9, Politics #11), while STEM subjects like Engineering and Computer Science show improvement trends. This report examines how these ranking dynamics interact with broader higher education industry trends globally, in Australia, and in South Asia.

4. Global Higher Education Trends

4.1 The rise of Asian competitors

The most significant global trend affecting ANU's ranking is the rapid ascent of Asian universities. The global education market is projected to reach US$10 trillion by 2030, with the highest growth in Central and Southern Asia, where tertiary enrolment increased 268% between 2000 and 2020. India alone added over 22,000 higher education institutions between 2006 and 2018, accounting for 74% of global growth in institution counts. Chinese universities now dominate several QS indicator categories, particularly citations per faculty and international research networks.

For ANU, this means that absolute improvements in research quality and reputation can still result in ranking declines when competitors improve faster. ANU's QS overall score of 87.4 in 2026 was its highest in six years, yet its rank dropped from #30 to #32.

4.2 Shift toward skills-based and career-aligned education

Globally, there is a structural shift away from traditional degree models toward micro-credentials, stackable credentials, and career-aligned programmes. According to Deloitte's 2025 Higher Education Trends report, 47% of Americans believe a four-year degree is worth the cost without loans, while 76% of trade school graduates consider their education worthwhile. The US is projected to see a 13% decline in college enrollment from 2025 through 2041.

This trend affects ANU's employer reputation and employment outcomes indicators (which account for 20% of the QS methodology). ANU's relatively academic, research-intensive profile is a strength in traditional metrics but may be challenged as ranking systems increasingly weight employability and practical outcomes.

4.3 International student mobility shifts

International student enrollments at American colleges fell 17% in fall 2025, with concerns about visa restrictions and a slowing global economy. This has created opportunities for Australian universities to attract students previously bound for the US, but also intensified competition among destination countries (UK, Canada, Australia, and increasingly Asian hubs like Malaysia and Singapore).

5. Australian Higher Education Trends

5.1 Australia-wide ranking decline

ANU's ranking decline is not isolated — it reflects a broader Australian trend. In the QS 2026 rankings, 69% of Australian universities declined in standing, compared to 60% for the UK and 31% for the US. Five of the eight Group of Eight universities dropped in rank. However, Australia remains third globally (behind the US and UK) in the number of institutions ranked in the top 50, top 100, and top 200.

5.2 International student composition

In YTD November 2025, 843,552 international students studied in Australia, a 0.4% decline from 2024. New student commencements declined by 15%. The top five source countries are China (23%), India (17%), Nepal (8%), Vietnam (4%), and the Philippines (4%). Three of these five — India, Nepal, and the Philippines — are South Asian or closely related markets, making these regional trends directly relevant to ANU's international student ratio (a QS indicator where ANU scores ~90/100).

5.3 ANU's size as a structural constraint

ANU is relatively small compared to other Go8 universities, with approximately 17,380 students (vs. Melbourne's 65,000+ or Sydney's 73,000+). ANU itself acknowledges that "being relatively smaller than other leading Australian universities provides a human-scale environment and a strong sense of community. However, this can work against us under some rankings criteria that prioritise scale." This particularly affects faculty-student ratio, employer reputation (fewer graduates in the workforce), and raw publication volumes.

6. South Asian Higher Education Trends

6.1 India's explosive institutional growth

India's gross enrolment ratio in higher education rose from 23.7% in 2014-15 to 28.4% in 2021-22, with total enrolment reaching 4.33 crore (43.3 million). India added over 22,000 institutions between 2006 and 2018. Several Indian institutions (particularly the IITs and IISc) have risen significantly in QS and THE rankings, directly competing with ANU in STEM subjects. This is reflected in QS's 2026 observation that "universities in Asia, particularly India, China, Malaysia and Thailand have demonstrated significant improvement."

6.2 Nepal and South Asia as student source markets

Nepal is Australia's third-largest student source country (8% of international students). South Asian students collectively represent a significant share of ANU's international cohort, influencing its international student ratio score. Any policy changes such as Australian visa restrictions or competition from emerging regional hubs directly impact this indicator. The 15% decline in new student commencements in Australia during 2025 is partly attributed to tighter student visa processing.

6.3 Research collaboration pathways

South Asian institutions are increasingly active in international research networks. ANU's international research network score (QS: ~85/100) could benefit from deepening collaboration with South Asian universities, particularly as the region's research output grows rapidly. The number of internationally mobile students from Central and Southern Asia has increased substantially, creating a larger pool of potential postgraduate researchers and collaborators.

8. Conclusion

ANU's ranking trajectory reflects the intersection of three forces: (1) intensifying global competition, especially from Asian universities benefiting from massive government investment and rapid institutional growth; (2) a broader weakening of Australian university rankings as a cohort, driven by relative declines in reputation and international outlook; and (3) ANU-specific structural factors, particularly its smaller size relative to peer institutions.
